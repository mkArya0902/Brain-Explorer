Neuroanatomy

Brain Anatomy

01

Cerebrum

Largest part of the brain — makes up about 85% of brain weight. Divided into left and right hemispheres, each controlling the opposite side of the body. Responsible for thinking, memory, speech, and voluntary movement.

Higher Functions
02

Cerebellum

Located at the back and bottom of the brain. Often called the "little brain." Controls balance, coordination, and fine motor movements. Damage causes ataxia — uncoordinated, clumsy movements.

Coordination
03

Brainstem

Connects the brain to the spinal cord. Consists of the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata. Controls vital functions like breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure. Damage can be life-threatening.

Vital Functions
04

Frontal Lobe

Anterior part of cerebrum. Responsible for personality, decision making, voluntary movement, and speech production (Broca's area). Prefrontal cortex is key for executive functions like planning and judgment.

Executive Function
05

Parietal Lobe

Located behind the frontal lobe. Processes sensory information — touch, pain, temperature, pressure. Contains the primary somatosensory cortex. Also involved in spatial awareness and navigation.

Sensory Processing
06

Temporal Lobe

Located on the sides of the brain near the temples. Processes hearing and language comprehension (Wernicke's area). Also important for memory formation — contains the hippocampus.

Memory & Hearing
07

Occipital Lobe

Located at the back of the brain. Primary visual processing center. Contains the visual cortex. Damage causes visual defects or even cortical blindness despite normal eyes.

Vision
Cortical Mapping

Functional Areas

Motor Cortex

Voluntary Movement
Controls all voluntary movement of the body. Located in the precentral gyrus of the frontal lobe. Body representation follows the "homunculus" map — hands and face have the largest areas.
📍 Frontal Lobe — Precentral Gyrus

Sensory Cortex

Touch & Pain Perception
Processes all somatosensory information — touch, pain, temperature. Located in the postcentral gyrus. Each body part has a dedicated region. Damage = loss of sensation (anesthesia).
📍 Parietal Lobe — Postcentral Gyrus

Broca's Area

Speech Production
Controls the formation and production of speech. Damage causes Broca's aphasia — patient understands language but speaks in short, broken sentences. Called "non-fluent aphasia."
📍 Left Frontal Lobe — Area 44, 45

Wernicke's Area

Language Understanding
Responsible for comprehension of language. Damage causes Wernicke's aphasia — patient speaks fluently but the words make no sense ("word salad"). Understanding is impaired.
📍 Left Temporal Lobe — Area 22

Visual Cortex

Vision Processing
Primary visual area (V1) receives signals from the eyes via the optic tract. Processes color, shape, movement, and depth. Located in the calcarine sulcus. Damage = visual field defects.
📍 Occipital Lobe — Calcarine Sulcus

Vascular Neurology

Brain Blood Supply

🔵

Circle of Willis

An anastomotic ring at the base of the brain formed by the junction of internal carotid and vertebral arteries. Provides collateral circulation — if one artery is blocked, blood can reroute through the circle. Major clinical significance in aneurysms and strokes.

🩸

Internal Carotid Arteries

Supply the anterior 2/3 of the brain including frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes. Gives rise to the anterior cerebral artery (ACA) and middle cerebral artery (MCA). MCA territory is the most common area for stroke.

🔴

Vertebrobasilar System

Two vertebral arteries join to form the basilar artery. Supplies the posterior brain — occipital lobe, cerebellum, and brainstem. Occlusion causes posterior circulation stroke with symptoms like vertigo, diplopia, and ataxia.

⚡

Clinical Note — Stroke

When blood supply to the brain is interrupted, neurons begin to die within 4–6 minutes. Two types: Ischemic stroke (clot blocks artery — 80% of cases) and Hemorrhagic stroke (vessel ruptures). Remember FAST — Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency. Treatment: tPA (clot buster) within 4.5 hours of ischemic stroke.

Clinical Neurology

Clinical Conditions

CONDITION 01

Stroke (CVA)

Cause
Ischemia (clot/thrombus) or hemorrhage in brain vessels. Risk factors: hypertension, diabetes, AF.
Symptoms
Sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech, visual loss, confusion, severe headache.
Complications
Paralysis, aphasia, dysphagia, aspiration pneumonia, DVT, depression.
Treatment
tPA (thrombolysis) within 4.5 hrs for ischemic stroke; surgical intervention for hemorrhagic.
CONDITION 02

Brain Tumor

Cause
Abnormal cell proliferation in brain tissue. Primary (glioma, meningioma) or secondary (metastatic).
Symptoms
Headache (worse in morning), nausea/vomiting, seizures, focal neurological deficits, personality change.
Complications
Raised ICP, brain herniation, hydrocephalus, cognitive decline.
Treatment
Surgery (craniotomy), radiotherapy, chemotherapy (temozolomide for GBM), steroids for edema.
CONDITION 03

Head Injury (TBI)

Cause
Trauma to the skull/brain — RTA, falls, assault. Classified as mild (concussion), moderate, or severe.
Symptoms
Loss of consciousness, amnesia, headache, vomiting, pupil changes, confusion (GCS score used).
Complications
Epidural/subdural hematoma, DAI, raised ICP, post-traumatic epilepsy, cognitive deficits.
Treatment
ABC stabilization, CT scan urgently, surgical evacuation of hematoma, ICP monitoring in ICU.

Brain Diseases

🧓

Alzheimer's Disease

Most common cause of dementia. Progressive destruction of neurons due to beta-amyloid plaques and tau tangles. Starts with short-term memory loss, progresses to complete cognitive failure. No cure — only symptomatic treatment (donepezil, memantine).

Neurodegenerative
🤲

Parkinson's Disease

Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra. Classic triad: tremor (pill-rolling, resting), rigidity (cogwheel), and bradykinesia. Also causes shuffling gait, mask-like face, micrographia. Treated with levodopa/carbidopa.

Neurodegenerative
⚡

Epilepsy

Recurrent, unprovoked seizures due to abnormal electrical activity. Focal (one area) or generalized (whole brain). Types: absence (petit mal), tonic-clonic (grand mal), temporal lobe epilepsy. Treated with antiepileptic drugs (sodium valproate, carbamazepine).

Seizure Disorder
🦠

Meningitis

Inflammation of the meninges (brain coverings). Bacterial (most dangerous — N. meningitidis), viral, or fungal. Classic triad: headache + neck stiffness (nuchal rigidity) + fever. Signs: Kernig's, Brudzinski's. Treat with IV antibiotics urgently — ceftriaxone.

Infectious / Inflammatory
